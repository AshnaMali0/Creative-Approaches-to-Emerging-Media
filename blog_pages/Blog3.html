Creative Approaches to Emerging Media

Blog 3

I went to the Museum of the Moving Image to see the dithering exhibit, and found it unremarkable, and I’m not even going to reflect on why I found it unremarkable.

Instead, I’ll talk about the exhibit going on in the other room: The Great Adventure of the Material World. I read the wall text first, which explains that the artist created this after a year of studying Buddhism and releasing no other works. The theater was set up to look like an arcade, and a looping video played. There were also two screens where visitors could play the game.

I played the game for a bit. The strengths of the game are its environments and enemy designs. I had trouble with the xbox controller (which I’m not good at) and had notes for the game design itself (character and dialog boxes would keep interrupting the flow).

The whole environment is reminiscent of an 80’s high-energy anime-style fighting game, but it’s immediately obvious that it’s a disguised dialogue on buddhist practices. The main character walks through the world and meditates on it, with all the naivete and curiosity of an 80’s video game character.

According to the articles we read, I’m hesitant to call this new media, since video games as an art form are not new. I will note that it deviates from typical video game structure by not having an end; instead, the game loops (following a life-death-rebirth cycle described in buddhist texts).

I liked this exhibition; I’m a fan of lengthy dialogues and character conversations, so I was engaged with the main character’s reflections. I don’t know what to make of the level designs, which are surreal landscapes. When I see a realistic video game landscape, I can immediately gauge how “good” the landscape is by how easy it is for me to get immersed and take the digital world for reality. For highly surreal landscapes, I don’t have the visual language to tell how good it is. The character lampshades this by commenting on how they are in such a strange world because it doesn’t follow the laws of physics they would normally expect from the world.

The wall text was cool for context on the artist, but I don’t think it was necessary to understand the art. Right off the bat the visual language of “80’s arcade game” is transparent, and after a short while, the audience gets the sense that the levels are designed on buddhist reflections.

All in all, I really like the medium of video games to slowly investigate and explore a world (and through them, buddhist lessons).
